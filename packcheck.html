No. 02

The Pack & Buy Guide

The pack is the one purchase that can make or break the trip, so it gets its own guide. Then the rest of the buy list. Borrow first — ask in the group thread. Prices approximate, July 2026.

First: fit is torso length, not age

A pack fits based on the distance from the C7 vertebra (the bony bump at the base of the neck when the chin drops) to the iliac crest (the line between the top of the hip bones). Measure every boy this week and bring the number to pack check. Typical ranges: 12–13 yrs ≈ 12–16" · 14–15 ≈ 15–18" · 16–17 ≈ 16–19" (adult small/medium).

BoyCan he use an adult pack?
16–17Yes — adult S/M fits fine; tons of used inventory.
14–15Usually — adjustable-torso adult packs on the shortest setting, or big-kid youth packs.
12–13Usually no. Most adult packs bottom out ~16–17" of torso (e.g. Osprey Rook: 17–22"). On a 14" torso the hip belt rides the ribs and the full load hangs on the shoulders — misery by mile 8. "Adjusting it smaller" can't shorten a frame longer than his spine. Use a youth-adjustable pack.

Second: it needs a frame — and here's the trap

The frame rule

  • ✅ Internal frame — the modern standard: a stay/framesheet in the back panel + padded hip belt that moves ~80% of the weight to the hips.
  • ✅ External frame — the old aluminum scout packs. Unfashionable, heavier, and genuinely fine; $20–40 used everywhere.
  • ❌ Frameless "50L hiking backpacks" online for $35–45 — book bags in costume. No load transfer, foam-strip hip belt. Fine for a sleepover, brutal for 22 miles.

The 10-second test: load 20 lb, buckle the hip belt snug on the hip bones, then unclip the shoulder straps entirely. A real pack still rides on the hips. A frameless pack falls off instantly. We'll run this test at pack check.

The move most people miss: rent

BYU Outdoors Unlimited (2201 N Canyon Rd, Provo · 801-422-2708 · outdoors.byu.edu) rents backpacking packs to the public at student-rec prices and can fit a boy in person — which solves the measuring problem entirely. For a 12-year-old who'll grow two torso sizes before the next trip, a rented pack that fits beats an owned pack that doesn't. August is peak season — reserve this week.

Weight Targets

Full pack = gear + share of group gear + food + water actually carried. Water is the sneaky one: 2.2 lb per liter — carry 2 L and refill often (lakes and creeks the whole way) instead of hauling more.

No. 04

How Pack Check Works

For the leaders running the night. Run it like a flight check, not a suggestion. Bring a bathroom or luggage scale. Every hiker — boys and dads — passes all eight gates before their pack is trip-approved. Dump-and-justify: everything comes out, and each item earns its way back in. (Gate 0, before the room opens: has the Forest Service permit call happened? See the alert on the trip guide.)

Gate 1Weigh-in

Loaded pack on the scale, with (empty) water bottles and a 2-lb stand-in for food. Target: ≤ 20–25% of body weight.

Gate 2Fit

Hip belt rides on the hip bones and carries ~80% of the load. Shoulder straps wrap without gaps. Sternum strap clipped.

Gate 3Shoes

Sturdy, broken-in, worn tonight. 12 days left is enough to break in — barely. No fresh-out-of-box shoes on Aug 10.

Gate 4Water

3–4 L of capacity (e.g. 2–3 L bladder + 1 L bottle). Show it. Bottles fit in reachable pockets.

Gate 5Sleep system

Bag rated 30°F or warmer-rated, pad, and a tent assignment. Bag lives inside a trash-compactor-bag liner.

Gate 6Storm ready

Rain jacket + warm layer (fleece/puffy) + beanie. Mountain storms don't care that it's August.

Gate 7Light & sound

Headlamp with fresh batteries, whistle reachable without taking the pack off.

Gate 8Walk test

Two laps around the parking lot, loaded. Watch for limping, strap-tugging, leaning. Fix it now, not at mile 4.
